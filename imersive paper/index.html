I. Introduction

The business industry encompasses a wide range of optimization problems, including the classical 0/1 knapsack problem, stock trend prediction [2], wireless sensor network deployment [4], and the traveling salesman problem (TSP). These problems often involve combinatorial decision-making, requiring the selection or exclusion of certain options. Additionally, some problems, such as the TSP, focus not only on which elements to select but also on determining their optimal order — for instance, deciding the sequence in which cities should be visited. Regardless of the specific problem type, the overarching objective is to minimize costs while maximizing overall benefits.

Metaheuristic algorithms are commonly utilized to handle these complex real-world applications. They may solve non-specific issues through some modification of encoding and achieve a near-optimal solution in a finite amount of time. Most metaheuristic algorithms are population- and iterative-based, and the solution gets better with the evolved process in iterations with efficient and effective searchability, such as the genetic algorithm (GA)[6], particle swarm optimization (PSO)[7], ant colony optimization (ACO)[8], estimation of the distribution algorithm (EDA)[9], quantum-inspired evolutionary algorithm (QEA)[10], and quantum-inspired tabu search algorithm (QTS)[11]. Various optimization problem-solving techniques have their own unique characteristics. Typically, the optimization search process of metaheuristic algorithms used to solve these problems is treated as a black box.

In recent years, the emerging topic of explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) [12], [13], [14] has been highly-valued. Users expect to clearly analyze the solving process to comprehend the efficacy and efficiency of optimization techniques. Therefore, the primary objective of XAI is to understand and interpret how the optimization techniques make decisions and derive the result, and then to increase the transparency of the optimization process. When analyzing the optimization process in solution space and how populations vary, users frequently output text information on the command line, log file, and Excel (Figure 1 and Figure 2) to improve the interpretability of the metaheuristics executing inner process. The expected observed goals are as follows: investigate and discover more details from the text including the algorithms' properties, patterns of movement, relationships between variables, and optimized trends among solutions. However, a large amount of information in a text lowers the perception of analyzing detail and makes it difficult to concentrate, which can lead to the omission of important pieces of information. Furthermore, the unorganized and messy information makes users hardly do the detailed analysis.

Visualization is an important process to make users gain insight into data from another perspective and comprehend the results more effectively. As the saying goes, “A picture is worth a thousand words.” Graphic representation constitutes one of the basic sign systems conceived by the human mind [15]. A picture can convey more than multiple ideas or a detailed text explanation. Therefore, this study presents an interface tool for the combinatorial optimization problem and evolutionary algorithm that is simpler to analyze the inner process of the optimization technique in a more visually interesting manner. To assist users in analyzing the process of metaheuristic algorithms and achieving the expected observed goals, this study proposes an innovative visualization tool for combinatorial optimization problems, Evo-Panel 2.0. Aside from visualizing a static picture, a video tells information more strongly than a still picture. Therefore, Evo-Panel 2.0 is designed with a dynamic interface, which can intuitively and vividly present the detailed process of an optimization algorithm through interactive animation. Most importantly, Evo-Panel 2.0 can visualize the optimized process of metaheuristics. The target of Evo-Panel 2.0 is to help users straightforwardly observe the solving process of the combinatorial problem.

Depending on the various properties of a combinatorial optimization problem, Evo-Panel 2.0 provides three different interfaces to visualize the combinatorial optimization problem, including general, deployment, and permutation-based combination optimization interfaces. The first is the general combinatorial optimization interface, which is used for general combinatorial optimization problems that have no sequential relation between each item, such as the 0/1 knapsack problem and stock selection problem. The second is for the deployment combinatorial optimization problem, which needs to accommodate the requirement of the map's requirement and minimize the sensor quantity and cost. The final is the permutation-based combinatorial optimization problem, in which each location has a sequential correlation and needs to minimize the path cost. In this problem, the solution is described by a permutation of n integers, such as TSP and minimum spanning tree that both need to minimize the path. Due to the characteristics of combinatorial optimization problems, the stochastic mechanism based on probability is frequently employed. Thus, in addition to the exact solution demonstration, Evo-Panel 2.0 offers the visualization of the probabilistic model's variation status, allowing users to acknowledge more detailed information easily and applicable to various metaheuristics. Evo-Panel 2.0 provides diverse and flexible visualization features and interfaces for different metaheuristics and different combinatorial optimization problems. This study aims to provide researchers with a useful visualization tool for analyzing the optimization technique, enhancing the interpretability of the optimized inner process, and assisting users in validating the design concept and program process consistency. The major contributions of Evo-Panel 2.0 are listed below.

  • Insight: With its dynamic visualization capabilities, Evo-Panel 2.0 provides an intuitive and detailed presentation of optimization algorithm processes. For instance, it demonstrates the algorithm's ability to escape local optima.
  • Versatility: Evo-Panel 2.0 offers flexibility, aiding educators in delivering vivid explanations of algorithms and optimization problems, enhancing students' comprehensive understanding in an engaging manner. Additionally, Evo-Panel 2.0 accommodates various types of metaheuristic algorithms and combinatorial optimization problems.
  • Support: Evo-Panel 2.0 can accelerate researchers' analysis efficiency and reduce their burden with vivid interfaces rather than watching dense text of algorithms' information. For example, Evo-Panel 2.0 can assist researchers in demonstrating whether the process of an algorithm complies with their ideas.
  • Innovation: It is a tool that can exactly and faithfully present the evolution of algorithms. Furthermore, visualization can help users improve and design algorithms.
  • Interactivity: Evo-Panel is an interactive dynamic visualization tool that incorporates the three essential elements of interactive design, interactive systems, and interactive built-in mechanisms, enhancing the usability and user engagement of the proposed visual aid in this research paper.

II. Optimization Problems

0/1 Knapsack Problem

To illustrate, we utilize the 0/1 knapsack problem as a benchmark case study. In this problem, the notation '1' represents selection, and '0' signifies non-selection. To maximize the total value, researchers attempt to find a set of items while adhering to a weight constraint. In essence, the knapsack aims to carry items that possess a favorable cost-to-performance ratio, all the while complying with weight restrictions. In order to facilitate comprehension, we have simplified the 0/1 knapsack problem. The items in this 0/1 knapsack problem are divided into ten types, and each type has ten items. The weights of the item types are 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, and 10; the values of the item types are 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, and 15, which are the weights plus 5. If the problem’s knapsack weight is large or small, it is easy to take all the items or remove all the items. Hence, we limit the weight constraint to half (275) of the total weight (550), so the problem becomes a non-trivial solution. Besides, the optimal solution (value) for this problem is 620, and the cost-performance (CP) value from high to low is from lowest weight to highest weight. For this kind of general combinatorial optimization problem, Evo-Panel 2.0 provides two models: general and probability models, to visualize the problem-solving process of metaheuristics.

Placement Optimization and Detection Probability Calculation

The deployment optimization problem, also referred to as the wireless sensor network (WSN) problem, has applications in various fields, such as home automation, traffic monitoring and control, remote robotic surgery in medicine, industrial machine surveillance, and environmental fire detection. It represents a specific and critical challenge within the domain of combinatorial optimization. The deployment optimization problem's solution space is a two-dimensional map in which the problem needs to use the sensor as little as possible while satisfying the limited detection probability at each position (threshold). In this paper, a map is composed of a two-dimensional grid ($g_{ij}$). If the size of the map is $m*n$, then its width is $m$ and its length is $n$. Each grid point can only accommodate one sensor (S) at most. After placing the sensor, the detection probability is calculated based on the related-distance probabilistic detection model [36]. The detection probability ($P_{g_{ij}}^{S_{xy}}$) decreases with increasing distance from the sensor ($S_{xy}$). The detecting model has a confident and maximum radius convergence circle ($R_{cof}$ and $R_{max}$) as shown in Figure 8.

$R_{cof}$ is smaller than $R_{max}$. The confident radius convergence circle is the range of the sensor that must be detected, while the maximum radius convergence circle is the range of the sensor that can be detected, with signal strength decreasing with increasing distance. The detection probability calculation is separated into three situations (Equation 1):

$$P^{S_{xy}}_{g_{ij}}= \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 1, & \text{if } \|S_{xy}g_{ij}\| < R_{cof} \\\\ \frac{1}{\|S_{xy}g_{ij}\|}, & \text{if } R_{cof} < \|S_{xy}g_{ij}\| \le R_{max} \\\\ 0, & \text{if } \|S_{xy}g_{ij}\| > R_{max} \end{array} \right.$$
(1)

If Euclidean distance between the sensors and the grid point ($‖S_{xy} g_{ij}‖$) is in the confident circle, the sensor must be detected; hence, the chance of detection is 1. When the $‖S_{xy} g_{ij}‖$ is beyond the maximum circle, which indicates that it is outside the sensing radius, the chance of detection is 0. If the distance between the sensors and the grid point falls within the maximum circle but not the confident circle, the detection probability is equal to the reciprocal of ($‖S_{xy} g_{ij}‖$). Here, if the grid point is detected by more than two sensors, Equation 2:

$$P^{S_{xy}}_{g_{ij}}=1-\left(\prod_{S \in \eta} \left(1-P_g\right)\right)$$
(2)

is used to calculate the detection probability, which means that the grid point is in the union of more than two sensors' $R_{max}$.

III. Proposed Visualization Tool

Within the domain of business, there are a multitude of optimization challenges that require resolution. Prior to resolving optimization challenges specific to the industry, researchers typically evaluate optimization algorithms using benchmark functions. Enhancing the efficacy of an optimization algorithm on benchmark functions increases the probability that it will successfully tackle optimization functions that are specific to a given industry. In this section, we use three benchmark problems to introduce how the system works on the three interfaces, including GA and QTS solving the 0/1 knapsack problem in the general combinatorial optimization interface, Entanglement-QTS [35] solving the deployment optimization problem in the deployment combinatorial optimization interface, and ACO solving TSP in the permutation/linear combinatorial optimization interface.

Evo-Panel 2.0 can show the fitness, all item combinations in each generation, and items' detailed information so that users can check if the metaheuristic matches their thoughts or problems' constraints.

The characteristic of the probability-based metaheuristic is that it uses probability to determine if the bit is chosen or not. Based on the probability characteristic, we proposed the probability model for analyzing the EC that has a probability matrix, such as QEA, EDA, and QTS. In order to make the probability change intuitively observable through color, the probability model uses the heatmap method, which uses a red-to-blue rainbow hue to present the bit's probability from 1 to 0 (Figure 6a).

An EC called QTS uses probability to measure whether or not to choose the bit. Here, we use QTS to solve the 0/1 knapsack problem as an example. At the beginning of QTS's evolution process, the probability of every bit is set to half (0.5), so the display screen is almost green. The chance of being chosen and not chosen is the same, so items are randomly chosen. As the number of iterations increases (from Figure 6a $\sim$ Figure 6d), the color gradually turns from green to yellow, orange, and red on the left side screen. This means that the chosen probability gradually increases, which is because the knapsack's cost-performance ratio is higher the farther to the left. On the other side, the color changes from green to darker green, light blue, and dark blue. By progressively decreasing the probability of being selected, QTS gradually eliminates the knapsack of the low cost-performance ratio on the right side. Through the change in color, we can clearly and intuitively identify the change in probability. In addition, the system provides “detailed information” for users to see each bit's probability (Figure 6e) if users want to check the exact number of probabilities. Clicking on the bit can see more information to analyze more precisely, including the bit's number, chosen or not, and probability.

When QTS ends (Figure 6f), we can see that the left side screen is full of red color and the blue color is almost on the right side. There are a few blue columns in the red area. From a different perspective, the global best (GB) in Figure 6f and the combinations in Figure 6g, three columns (bits) that point by the arrow are not chosen. To find the best solution to this problem, the algorithm discards items with a high cost-performance ratio and picks the items with a lower cost-performance ratio. This is because the 0/1 knapsack problem's knapsack has entanglement (dependency) between each item under some constraints. That is why the 0/1 knapsack problem is an NP-complete problem and why it is interesting. In addition, in this 0/1 knapsack problem, the best combination is more than one. During the analysis in Figure 6h, we can see that the algorithm finds a different global best solution.

Deployment Optimization Interface

1.Function Introduction

Deployment problems need a comprehensive view of the solution space because each position affects its neighborhood. The whole map of the solution space in Evo-Panel 2.0's deployment interface can help users observe the effect of the sensor on the surrounding position through the color change.

In the deployment combinatorial optimization interface as shown in Figure 8b, the map size is dependent on the user's setting or the problem's constraint. One grid point denotes one place. The black color represents the sensor, while the red-to-blue rainbow hue indicates the system-calculated sensor detection probability from 1 to 0. For ease of presentation, we set $R_{cof}=1$, $R_{max}=5$, where The detectable range is a circular area. In Figure 8b, the map size is 9$\times$9, and a sensor is placed at the center of the map. The probability of detection ($P_{g_{ij}}^{S_{xy}}$) increases as the grid point is closer to the sensor. Conversely, it decreases as the distance from the sensor increases. When a grid point falls within the detection range of multiple sensors, as demonstrated by the center grid in Figure 8c, its probability of detection increases according to Equation 2 (the probability increase from 0.333 to 0.556). The intriguing aispect of the deployment optimzation problem lies in strategically positioning the minimum number of sensors to ensure mutual reinforcement, thereby enabling all grid points on the map to achieve a probability higher than the specified threshold.

You can interact with the map by clicking on grid points to add or remove sensors and toggle between the "Detection Probability" and "Threshold" views using the corresponding button. The goal is to place the minimum number of sensors required to ensure all grid points meet the minimum threshold 0.3.

As the map size increases in the deployment optimization problem, achieving the threshold for each grid point relies more heavily on the mutual support between sensors. For example, Figure 8d demonstrates maps of varying sizes, including 5$\times$5, 10$\times$10, and 20$\times$20 grids. Each grid point is assigned a uniform threshold of 0.3. Building on the insights from Figure 8d, we analyze the sensor requirements for maps of different sizes to ensure all grid points meet the threshold. In a 5$\times$5 map, a single sensor is sufficient to satisfy the threshold for all positions. When the map size is doubled to 10$\times$10, at least four sensors are required to achieve the same threshold satisfaction. For a 20$\times$20 map, achieving the threshold for each grid point increasingly depends on the mutual support between sensors, as overlapping detection areas allow for redundancy reduction. While 16 sensors are initially required to satisfy the threshold for all positions, the optimization process leverages sensor overlap to reduce this number. Consequently, only 12 sensors are needed to ensure all grid points meet the threshold, demonstrating the efficiency of the deployment optimization strategy.

Expanding the scope of the analysis, we explore the deployment optimization problem on a larger 50$\times$50 map, where the complexity increases exponentially with the map size. Unlike smaller maps, where sensor placement can be relatively straightforward, larger maps require metaheuristic algorithms to efficiently navigate the vast solution space. Permutation Combinatorial Optimization Interface

In combinatorial optimization problems, solutions are often represented as permutations, as exemplified by the classic Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP). In the TSP, a fixed number of cities are involved, each with its own distinct spatial coordinates. Consider a TSP instance with $n$ cities, wherein the objective is for the salesman to visit each city precisely once before returning to the starting point. The computational complexity of this problem increases factorially with the number of locations, posing a challenge in devising strategies to minimize time and cost. Evo-Panel 2.0 features a dedicated interface tailored for permutation optimization problems, specifically designed for addressing challenges like those posed by TSP. Our interface offers a comprehensive view of the entire route and evolutionary progression, alongside numerous functionalities such as zooming and pausing, facilitating in-depth analysis.

1.Function Introduction

Evo-Panel 2.0 can display the algorithmic process in permutation and linear problems. Taking TSP solving Burma 14 (one of TSP problems in the TSP library [37]) as an example, the system draws the user-specified map, x1- and x2-axis coordinated positions, and every location on the map (Figure 10a). The system displays both the solution space and the algorithm's process in the middle. The black dot represents all the locations (cities) in Burma 14, which has 14 cities that the salesman must visit. When the algorithm begins, the system draws the road in a red-to-blue hue based on distance. The distance corresponds with the color bar on the right-hand side of the screen. The color is closer to red if the distance is longer and closer to blue if the distance is shorter. Evo-Panel 2.0 displays the changes in roads as the number of iterations increases (from Figure 10a to Figure 10d). Researchers can easily determine whether the metaheuristic changed the path or which shortest road was found by the metaheuristic. At the beginning of ant colony optimization (ACO), some roads are red or orange because the metaheuristic has not yet discovered shorter paths. As the metaheuristic continues, the red line decreases (Figure 10b). When the evolutionary process goes to the post-period, as depicted in Figure 10c, most roads are green, which means the metaheuristic found an optimal path. At the end of the evolutionary process (Figure 10d), metaheuristics find the shortest path in Burma 14.

After outlining the ACO process, we proceed to introduce several functionalities within the permutation interface. In Figure 10e, we deactivate the “aligned grid line” while activating the "path usage" feature to enhance graph visibility. The "aligned grid line" serves as an auxiliary guide for city alignment, while the “path usage” function provides insights into road alterations. By comparing current and prior paths, it enables an analysis of metaheuristic trends. The varying shades of gray denote the recency of road usage, allowing users to discern the most recently traversed routes. Lighter shades indicate older generations, whereas darker shades signify more recent generations. In Figure 10e, the track fades and becomes whiter with the time of metaheuristic not go, indicating that the ant's pheromone volatilizes over time. Researchers can analyze if the metaheuristic expends computational time redundantly, thereby enabling the identification of areas with potential for enhancement. Besides, the interface in Evo-Panel 2.0 can be "zoomed in" and "moved" to the area of interest (Figure 10f) to analyze two nearby locations. “Zoom-in” and “zoom-out” allow users to analyze the solution space with greater detail and a broader perspective. If you move the mouse over a city, our visualization tool can display information about the city, including the city's name and the location of the x1 and x2 axes. When we move the mouse over these two nearby cities in Figure 10f, the system shows that their x2-axis coordinates are identical. After observing particular regions, we can “reset solution space” to quickly return to the initial solution space (Figure 10g). After adjusting the solution space through zooming in and out, it is simple to reset it with one click. In the end, we can view “detailed information” regarding the length of all paths (Figure 10h), allowing us to carefully examine each path and conduct additional research.

Evo-Panel 2.0 is a powerful analysis tool for benchmark functions as well as real-world applications of various combinatorial optimization problems. This subsection uses GNQTS to solve the stock selection problem as an example. The stock selection problem is a real-world application of a general combinatorial optimization problem. In this stock selection example, one bit corresponds to one stock. The combinatorial is composed of the NASDAQ 100 index of the U.S. stock market. The trend ratio [40] guides the algorithm in its search for a portfolio with a high-return and low-risk profile.

GNQTS is a probability-based metaheuristic for combinatorial optimization problems, which means it chooses stocks based on the probability of each stock. The guiding principle behind GNQTS involves the pursuit of favorable stocks while actively evading unfavorable ones. In the early stage, the chosen probability is set to half (0.5), and the color is around green in Figure 11a, so the portfolio in Figure 11b is staggered (the black and white colors are staggered). As the iteration goes, Figure 11c selects fewer stocks than Figure 11b. Additionally, the detailed information can assist users in observing and analyzing which stock was selected by metaheuristic. Here, Figure 11c depicts the portfolio from $181^{th}$ to $191^{th}$ generation. Corresponding to Figure 11d, the “detailed information” indicates which NASDAQ 100 stock is selected. As the research progresses to the intermediate stage (Figure 11e), the algorithm finds the trend and increases the probability of the best portfolio, so the probability starts moving to orange or light blue colors, even red or blue colors.

In Figure 11f, a single stock is chosen by the global best portfolio, prompting GNQTS to utilize the quantum-not gate for navigating away from local optima (as depicted by the shift from blue to red within the yellow box). As the algorithm progresses towards completion (Figure 11g), the colors transition to dark blue or dark red. Consequently, as illustrated in the probability plot in Figure 11g, the algorithm discards stocks with a probability of 0 (shown in dark blue) while retaining favorable stocks with a chosen probability of 1 (depicted in dark red) in Figure 11h. The convergence curves in Figure 11i serve as a concise representation of the algorithm's performance, offering insights into the moments of discovering a promising portfolio and the overall trend during the quest for the optimal solution.

Through the use of the Evo-Panel to analyze algorithms for solving stock selection problems, researchers can acquire additional algorithmic insights from the visual interface. This includes evaluating whether the problem-solving process aligns with the designated methodology, tracking the evolution of the global best portfolio, and determining the convergence status of the algorithm, among other aspects. All this information contributes significantly to the effective refinement and enhancement of metaheuristics.

The threshold could be based on historical data such as the occurrence probability of fires, crimes, automobile accidents, traffic jams, etc. To handle a variety of situations, the map can be created with varying sizes, and each grid point can define the limit-detecting probability (threshold) individually.

In the deployment optimization case study, we investigate the deployment optimization problem within the context of three specified hotspots as delineated in Figure 12a. Arrangement Optimization Interface

In recent years, a well-developed network and the COVID-19 pandemic have contributed to the widespread adoption of online platforms for ordering food or groceries, such as food delivery and online shopping platforms. To deliver goods to consumers as soon as possible while minimizing costs, the delivery company needs to plan the shortest route possible. Therefore, finding the quickest route in real-world TSP applications is greatly regarded by businesses and consumers. However, the complexity of the TSP problem increases factorially with n location. The computer has its limitations and cannot quickly find the optimal solution. Through the visualization tool Evo-Panel 2.0, users can intuitively analyze the results of metaheuristics. With the interactive function, users can further improve the algorithm's output or generate their own path to minimize costs.

Evo-Panel 2.0 is an interactive visualization tool as well. We provide an interactive function that enables users to connect or modify the line to create or enhance a path. The user could start with an empty map (Figure 13a) or the result of metaheuristics (Figure 13b) to solve the TSP problem of att48 [37], which is 48 capitals of the US. Users can begin "recording" in either Figure 13a or Figure 13b to record the change process. If users begin with an empty map similar to Figure 13a, they could connect each city/capital one by one and return to the starting city. If users begin with the result of the metaheuristic shown in Figure 13b, they can delete the road connection and reconnect new roads whatever they want. The improved final result is depicted in Figure 13c, which represents the shortest path for this problem [37]. Once the process is completed, we can stop recording, take a picture, and save the improved result (Figure 13d). Furthermore, researchers can use the results to conduct additional research, create reports, and compose papers.

V. Conclusion

Evo-Panel 2.0 stands as an invaluable and precise tool, providing dynamic visual representations of metaheuristic algorithms solving combinatorial optimization problems. Firstly, it replaces extensive textual data with a visualization interface, streamlining algorithms and optimization problem analyses through vivid graphical depictions. Visualization expedites analytical processes, renders tedious tasks more engaging, deepens user comprehension of metaheuristic concepts and optimization problems, and liberates mental resources for future algorithmic enhancements. Secondly, Evo-Panel 2.0 offers versatile interfaces catering to distinct characteristics of combinatorial optimization problems, including general 'choose or not' interfaces, deployment interfaces for solutions with strong dependencies, and permutation interfaces for sequential solutions. Additionally, it incorporates a probability model tailored for probabilistic metaheuristics. Thirdly, our visualization tool offers interactive features, such as media player control bars for meticulous algorithmic observation and sensor manipulation within the deployment interface to refine metaheuristic solutions. Fourthly, Evo-Panel 2.0's compatibility with all metaheuristics and optimization technologies, sans the need for specific programming knowledge for analysis file generation, underscores its flexibility. Lastly, the visualization tool helps eXplainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) by making it easier to understand how computational intelligence works and revealing hidden insights that can help developers make better, easier to understand metaheuristics that can be used to solve a wide range of business optimization problems.
